Coordination compounds & Werner's theory
C
Werner isolated only 2 isomers of [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl from the reaction mixture. Using this experimental fact, explain how Werner ruled out the planar and trigonal prismatic geometries and proved the octahedral structure.
▼
Full explanation

Werner reasoned from the number of isomers backwards. For a compound MA₄B₂, the number of geometric isomers depends entirely on the geometry around the central metal:

Planar geometry → 3 possible arrangements for MA₄B₂ Trigonal prism → 3 possible arrangements Octahedral → only 2 arrangements (cis and trans)

Since Werner could only ever isolate 2 isomers of [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl experimentally, planar and trigonal prismatic geometries were eliminated — they would predict 3 isomers. Only the octahedral geometry accounts for exactly 2.

He further confirmed this with MA₃B₃ compounds (e.g. [Co(NH₃)₃Cl₃]): octahedral predicts exactly 2 isomers (facial where all 3 B are on one triangular face, and meridional where 3 B span a meridional plane). Werner isolated exactly 2, confirming octahedral.

Exam tip: always state the predicted isomer count for each geometry, then contrast with the experimental observation. The logic is purely deductive — ruling out geometries by elimination.
C
Explain why [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃ gives 3 moles of AgCl with AgNO₃, but [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl gives only 1 mole. How does this relate to Werner's primary and secondary valency?
▼
Werner's valency theory

Primary valency is the oxidation state of the metal — it is ionisable and must be satisfied by anions outside the coordination sphere. Secondary valency is the coordination number — it is non-ionisable and must be satisfied by ligands directly bonded inside.

[Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃: Primary valency = 3 → 3 Cl⁻ outside coordination sphere → ionisable → 3 AgCl with AgNO₃; total ions in solution = 4 (1 complex cation + 3 Cl⁻) [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl: Primary valency = 3 → only 1 Cl⁻ outside (satisfies 1 primary valency unit) 2 Cl⁻ are inside coordination sphere as ligands (satisfy secondary valency) → only 1 AgCl with AgNO₃; total ions = 2
The inner Cl⁻ in [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl simultaneously acts as a ligand AND as a charge-balance ion — this is the key dual role. The outer Cl⁻ only balances charge and is freely ionisable.

Crystal field theory & CFSE
C
Explain why d–d transitions in octahedral complexes are "Laporte forbidden" yet still produce colour. Why are tetrahedral complexes more intensely coloured than octahedral?
▼
Selection rules and colour intensity

Laporte rule: transitions between orbitals of the same parity (both gerade: g→g, or both ungerade: u→u) are forbidden. In an octahedral field, all 5 d-orbitals are gerade (g). Therefore d→d means g→g → Laporte forbidden.

Despite this, octahedral complexes still show colour (weakly) because vibronic coupling occurs: molecular vibrations (particularly asymmetric modes) temporarily remove the centre of symmetry. During these brief distortions, the Laporte rule is momentarily relaxed, allowing weak d–d transitions. This is why octahedral d–d bands are pale (low molar absorptivity, ε ≈ 5–50 L mol⁻¹ cm⁻¹).

Tetrahedral complexes (e.g. [CoCl₄]²⁻): → NO centre of symmetry (T_d point group) → d and p orbitals of the SAME symmetry species mix freely → d-orbitals gain partial p-character → transitions are effectively d→(d+p) which has Δl ≠ 0 → allowed → ε ≈ 200–1000 L mol⁻¹ cm⁻¹ → intense, dark colour [Co(H₂O)₆]²⁺: pale pink (ε ≈ 5) [CoCl₄]²⁻: dark blue (ε ≈ 600)
The number of photons absorbed = number of transitions. More allowed transitions → more intense absorption → darker, richer colour.

Step-by-step CFSE calculations
(a) [Mn(CN)₆]⁴⁻ — Mn²⁺, d⁵, low spin, octahedral
Mn²⁺ → d⁵ Low spin → t₂g⁵ eₘ⁰ (all 5 in t₂g, 2 pairs + 1 single) CFSE = 5 × (−4Dq) + 0 × (+6Dq) = −20Dq = −2.0Δₒ Pairing energy correction: Free ion d⁵ (HS): t₂g³ eₘ² → 0 pairs Low spin d⁵: t₂g⁵ → 2 pairs Extra pairs = 2 → add +2P Final CFSE = −20Dq + 2P

(c) [Co(CN)₆]⁴⁻ — Co²⁺, d⁷, low spin, octahedral

For Ni²⁺ (d⁸): (a) calculate CFSE in both octahedral and tetrahedral fields in units of Dqₒₒₜ, (b) calculate the octahedral site preference energy (OSPE), (c) explain why Ni²⁺ with CN⁻ forms square planar but with Cl⁻ forms tetrahedral. numerical

Ni²⁺ CFSE comparison
(a) Octahedral Ni²⁺ (d⁸): Config: t₂g⁶ eₘ² CFSE = 6×(−4Dq) + 2×(+6Dq) = −24 + 12 = −12Dq_oct Tetrahedral Ni²⁺ (d⁸): Inverted splitting: e set lower (dz², dx²-y²), t₂ set higher e⁴ t₂⁴ configuration CFSE = 4×(−6Dq_tet) + 4×(+4Dq_tet) = −24Dq_tet + 16Dq_tet = −8Dq_tet Using (Dq_tet) = (4/9)(Dq_oct): = −8 × (4/9) Dq_oct = −(32/9) Dq_oct ≈ −3.56 Dq_oct (b) OSPE = CFSE(oct) − CFSE(tet) = −12Dq_oct − (−3.56Dq_oct) = −8.44 Dq_oct Ni²⁺ gains ~8.4 Dq more stabilisation per mole in oct than tet
Why CN⁻ → square planar but Cl⁻ → tetrahedral

Two competing factors determine geometry:

CFSE factor: Square planar has the largest energy gap (dx²-y² pushed very high). Strong-field ligands like CN⁻ create large Δₒ → splitting large enough that sq. planar CFSE > tet CFSE. With CN⁻, CFSE factor dominates → [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ square planar, diamagnetic.

Size factor: Cl⁻ is a large ligand. Steric repulsion between 4 large Cl⁻ ligands in square planar (all in same plane, 90° apart) is severe. Tetrahedral geometry (109.5°) relieves this repulsion. For Cl⁻, size factor overrides CFSE → [NiCl₄]²⁻ tetrahedral, paramagnetic (2 unpaired, μ = 2.83 BM).

C
State and explain the three main factors that affect the magnitude of Δₒ (10Dq). Illustrate with the example [V(H₂O)₆]³⁺ vs [V(H₂O)₆]²⁺.
▼
Factors affecting Δₒ

1. Nature of ligand (spectrochemical series): Strong-field ligands (CO, CN⁻) cause large splitting; weak-field (Cl⁻, Br⁻, H₂O) cause small splitting. CN⁻ can π-backbond with metal d-orbitals (accepting electron density into π* orbitals) → enhances splitting dramatically.

Weak: I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < H₂O Moderate: NH₃ < en Strong: NO₂⁻ < CN⁻ ≈ CO

2. Charge of the metal ion: Higher oxidation state → metal nucleus pulls ligands closer → ligand field acts more strongly → larger Δₒ. [V(H₂O)₆]³⁺ has Δₒ significantly greater than [V(H₂O)₆]²⁺ because V³⁺ is smaller and pulls water ligands nearer, intensifying the field.

3. Period/nature of the metal (3d < 4d < 5d): Heavier metals have more diffuse, spatially extended d-orbitals that overlap better with ligand orbitals → larger splitting. Same charge: Co³⁺ < Rh³⁺ < Ir³⁺. This is why 4d and 5d metals almost always form low-spin complexes regardless of ligand.

Using CFSE arguments, determine and justify whether Fe₃O₄, Mn₃O₄, and Co₃O₄ adopt normal or inverse spinel structures. Note: O²⁻ is a weak-field ligand throughout. challenging
▼
Systematic CFSE-based spinel analysis

In spinel AB₂O₄, O²⁻ (weak field) means all complexes are HIGH SPIN. The ion with greater octahedral site preference (larger OSPE) occupies the octahedral holes.

(1) Fe₃O₄ = Fe²⁺Fe₂³⁺O₄ Fe³⁺ (d⁵ HS): CFSE(oct) = 3(−4)+2(+6) = 0; CFSE(tet) = 0 → OSPE = 0 Fe²⁺ (d⁶ HS): CFSE(oct) = 4(−4)+2(+6) = −4Dq; CFSE(tet) ≈ −1.6Dq OSPE(Fe²⁺) = −4 − (−1.6) = −2.4Dq_oct (oct preferred) Fe²⁺ strongly prefers octahedral → takes oct holes Fe³⁺ has no preference → one goes to tet, one to oct → INVERSE SPINEL: [Fe³⁺]tet [Fe²⁺Fe³⁺]oct O₄ ✓
(2) Mn₃O₄ = Mn²⁺Mn₂³⁺O₄ Mn³⁺ (d⁴ HS): CFSE(oct) = 3(−4)+1(+6) = −6Dq CFSE(tet) = 3(−6)+1(+4) Dq_tet; scale to oct: approx −2.7Dq_oct OSPE(Mn³⁺) = −6 − (−2.7) = −3.3Dq_oct → prefers oct Mn³⁺ occupies oct holes → NORMAL SPINEL ✓
(3) Co₃O₄ = Co²⁺Co₂³⁺O₄ Co³⁺ (d⁶): Co³⁺ is a special case. Despite O²⁻ being weak field, Co³⁺ almost ALWAYS goes low spin because its intrinsically large Δₒ (3d series position) exceeds P. Exceptions: ONLY [CoF₆]³⁻ and [CoF₃(H₂O)₃] are high spin. Low-spin Co³⁺: CFSE(oct) = 6(−4)+0(+6) = −24Dq → huge oct preference → Co³⁺ takes ALL oct holes → NORMAL SPINEL ✓ (Calculation would predict inverse; experiment says normal) → This is the classic exception to note!
Fe³⁺ and Mn²⁺ (both d⁵ HS) always have CFSE = 0 in BOTH sites — they never decide spinel type. The deciding ion is always the one with non-zero CFSE.
Colour & spectra
C
KMnO₄ is intensely purple while [Co(H₂O)₆]²⁺ is only pale pink. Both absorb visible light — explain why there is such a dramatic difference in intensity using selection rules.
▼
Charge transfer vs d-d transition intensity

KMnO₄ — charge transfer band (intense): The absorption arises from ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT): electron transfers from filled O²⁻ p-orbitals to empty Mn 3d orbitals (Mn is +7, highly electron-deficient). This is a p→d transition (Δl = 1) — fully Laporte-allowed. Additionally, ΔS = 0 is satisfied. Both selection rules are obeyed → very high molar absorptivity (ε ~ 10⁴ L mol⁻¹ cm⁻¹).

[Co(H₂O)₆]²⁺ — d-d transition (weak): Absorption is from t₂g → eₘ within the d-subshell. This is a d→d transition (Δl = 0) — Laporte forbidden. Spin selection rule (ΔS = 0) is satisfied. But the Laporte-forbidden nature means the transition is only weakly allowed via vibronic coupling (vibrations briefly disturb octahedral symmetry). ε ~ 5–50 L mol⁻¹ cm⁻¹ → pale colour.

Intensity hierarchy: Spin-forbidden d–d: ε ~ 0.01–1 Spin-allowed d–d (oct): ε ~ 5–50 Spin-allowed d–d (tet): ε ~ 50–500 (Laporte relaxed) Charge transfer (LMCT/MLCT): ε ~ 10³–10⁵ (fully allowed)

2

N
Calculate the potential of the Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ couple when Fe³⁺ is complexed by H₃PO₄. Given: when [Fe³⁺] is reduced to 10⁻⁶ M due to complexation and [Fe²⁺] = 0.1 M, find E. Why does this matter for dichrometry? [E°(Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺) = 0.77 V] numerical

Half-reaction: Fe³⁺ + e⁻ → Fe²⁺, n = 1 Nernst: E = E° + (0.059/1) × log([Fe³⁺]/[Fe²⁺]) E = 0.77 + 0.059 × log(10⁻⁶ / 0.1) = 0.77 + 0.059 × log(10⁻⁵) = 0.77 + 0.059 × (−5) = 0.77 − 0.295 = 0.475 V

N
Construct the titration of 25 mL of 0.1 M CH₃COOH with 0.1 M NaOH. Calculate the pH at: (a) start, (b) half-equivalence point, (c) equivalence point, (d) after adding 30 mL NaOH. [Ka(CH₃COOH) = 1.8×10⁻⁵; Kw = 10⁻¹⁴] numerical
▼
Weak acid–strong base titration curve
(a) Start — pure 0.1 M CH₃COOH
CH₃COOH ⇌ CH₃COO⁻ + H⁺, Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵ [H⁺] = √(Ka × C) = √(1.8×10⁻⁵ × 0.1) = √(1.8×10⁻⁶) = 1.342×10⁻³ M pH = −log(1.342×10⁻³) = 2.87
(b) Half-equivalence point — 12.5 mL NaOH added
At half-equivalence: [CH₃COOH] = [CH₃COO⁻] Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) = pKa + log(1) = pKa pH = −log(1.8×10⁻⁵) = 4.74 (This is also the most effective buffer point)
(c) Equivalence point — 25 mL NaOH added
All acetic acid converted to CH₃COONa (weak base hydrolysis) Total volume = 50 mL; [CH₃COO⁻] = (0.1×25)/50 = 0.05 M Kb = Kw/Ka = 10⁻¹⁴ / 1.8×10⁻⁵ = 5.56×10⁻¹⁰ [OH⁻] = √(Kb × C) = √(5.56×10⁻¹⁰ × 0.05) = √(2.78×10⁻¹¹) = 5.27×10⁻⁶ pOH = 5.28 → pH = 14 − 5.28 = 8.72 (pH > 7 at equivalence — alkaline, as expected for weak acid/strong base)
(d) After 30 mL NaOH — excess base region
Excess NaOH = (30 − 25) mL × 0.1 M = 0.5 mmol excess OH⁻ Total volume = 25 + 30 = 55 mL [OH⁻] = 0.5 / 55 = 9.09×10⁻³ M (excess NaOH dominates) pOH = −log(9.09×10⁻³) = 2.04 pH = 14 − 2.04 = 11.96
Indicator selection: phenolphthalein (pH 8.3–10) is suitable — it changes colour right around the equivalence point pH of 8.72. Methyl orange (3.1–4.4) would be unsuitable — the equivalence point is far outside its range.
N
Calculate the pH of: (a) 0.01 M HCl, (b) 0.01 M NaOH, (c) a buffer made from 0.1 M CH₃COOH + 0.15 M CH₃COONa, (d) 0.05 M NH₄Cl solution. [Ka(CH₃COOH)=1.8×10⁻⁵; Kb(NH₃)=1.8×10⁻⁵; Kw=10⁻¹⁴] numerical
▼
Four pH calculations
(a) 0.01 M HCl — strong acid, fully ionised [H⁺] = 0.01 = 10⁻² M pH = 2.00 (b) 0.01 M NaOH — strong base, fully ionised [OH⁻] = 0.01 = 10⁻² M → pOH = 2 pH = 14 − 2 = 12.00 (c) Buffer: 0.1 M CH₃COOH + 0.15 M CH₃COONa Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) pKa = −log(1.8×10⁻⁵) = 4.74 pH = 4.74 + log(0.15/0.1) = 4.74 + log(1.5) = 4.74 + 0.176 = 4.92 (d) 0.05 M NH₄Cl — salt of weak base + strong acid → acidic NH₄⁺ hydrolyses: NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺ Kh = Kw/Kb = 10⁻¹⁴ / 1.8×10⁻⁵ = 5.56×10⁻¹⁰ [H⁺] = √(Kh × C) = √(5.56×10⁻¹⁰ × 0.05) = √(2.78×10⁻¹¹) = 5.27×10⁻⁶ pH = −log(5.27×10⁻⁶) = 5.28 (acidic, as expected)
N
A polyprotic acid H₃PO₄ (0.1 M). Given pKa₁=2.15, pKa₂=7.20, pKa₃=12.35. (a) Calculate the pH of 0.1 M H₃PO₄ (use only Ka₁). (b) What volume of 0.1 M NaOH is needed to reach the second equivalence point starting from 25 mL H₃PO₄? (c) At the first equivalence point, what is the pH? [Ka₁=7.08×10⁻³] numerical
▼
(a) pH of 0.1 M H₃PO₄: Ka₁ = 7.08×10⁻³ (dominant ionisation) [H⁺] = √(Ka₁ × C) = √(7.08×10⁻³ × 0.1) = √(7.08×10⁻⁴) = 0.02661 M pH = −log(0.02661) = 1.58 (b) Volume of NaOH to second equivalence point: First equivalence: H₃PO₄ + NaOH → NaH₂PO₄ (1:1 ratio) Second equivalence: NaH₂PO₄ + NaOH → Na₂HPO₄ (another 1:1) Total NaOH needed = 2 × moles of H₃PO₄ moles H₃PO₄ = 0.1 × 0.025 = 2.5×10⁻³ mol moles NaOH needed = 5×10⁻³ mol Volume = 5×10⁻³ / 0.1 = 0.05 L = 50 mL (c) At first equivalence point: solution is NaH₂PO₄ H₂PO₄⁻ is amphoteric (can donate or accept H⁺) pH = (pKa₁ + pKa₂) / 2 = (2.15 + 7.20) / 2 = 4.68
For an amphoteric species (H₂PO₄⁻, HPO₄²⁻), pH = (pKa_n + pKa_{n+1}) / 2. This is a clean result that does not depend on concentration (valid when Ka's are not too different).
N
Calculate the solubility of AgCl in: (a) pure water, (b) 0.1 M NaCl solution (common ion effect), (c) 0.1 M AgNO₃. Comment on what you observe. [Ksp(AgCl) = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰] numerical
▼
(a) Solubility in pure water: AgCl ⇌ Ag⁺ + Cl⁻, Ksp = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻] = s² s = √Ksp = √(1.8×10⁻¹⁰) = 1.34×10⁻⁵ mol/L (b) Solubility in 0.1 M NaCl (common ion Cl⁻): Ksp = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻] = s × (s + 0.1) ≈ s × 0.1 (since s << 0.1) 1.8×10⁻¹⁰ = s × 0.1 s = 1.8×10⁻⁹ mol/L (c) Solubility in 0.1 M AgNO₃ (common ion Ag⁺): Ksp = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻] = (s + 0.1) × s ≈ 0.1 × s s = 1.8×10⁻⁹ mol/L (same result, same common ion concentration) Comparison: Pure water: s = 1.34×10⁻⁵ mol/L (= 1.92 mg/L) 0.1 M NaCl: s = 1.8×10⁻⁹ mol/L (10,000× less soluble) 0.1 M AgNO₃: s = 1.8×10⁻⁹ mol/L (10,000× less soluble)

The common ion effect reduces solubility by ~4 orders of magnitude. This is the basis for gravimetric analysis — adding excess AgNO₃ or Cl⁻ ensures essentially complete precipitation.

N
Calculate the degree of hydrolysis and pH of 0.01 M CH₃COONa solution. [Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵; Kw = 10⁻¹⁴] numerical
▼
Salt of weak acid (CH₃COOH) + strong base (NaOH) → alkaline hydrolysis CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻ Kh = Kw / Ka = 10⁻¹⁴ / 1.8×10⁻⁵ = 5.56×10⁻¹⁰ Degree of hydrolysis h = √(Kh / C) = √(5.56×10⁻¹⁰ / 0.01) = √(5.56×10⁻⁸) = 2.36×10⁻⁴ h = 0.0236% (very small, as expected for a common salt) [OH⁻] = h × C = 2.36×10⁻⁴ × 0.01 = 2.36×10⁻⁶ M pOH = −log(2.36×10⁻⁶) = 5.63 pH = 14 − 5.63 = 8.37 (alkaline ✓)
Alternatively: [OH⁻] = √(Kh × C) = √(5.56×10⁻¹²) = 2.36×10⁻⁶ M. Same result — the degree of hydrolysis and [OH⁻] routes both work.

25 mL of 0.1 M acetic acid was titrated with 0.1 M NaOH. (a) Select the appropriate indicator and justify using pH range calculations. (b) Calculate the pH at 20 mL of NaOH added. (c) Calculate the change in pH per mL of NaOH added near the equivalence point (at 24.9 mL and 25.1 mL). [Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵] numerical challenging
▼
(a) Indicator selection

Equivalence point pH = 8.72 (weak acid/strong base, calculated above). Phenolphthalein changes colour from pH 8.3 to 10.0. Since equivalence point (8.72) falls within this range, phenolphthalein is correct. Methyl orange (3.1–4.4) is completely wrong — the equivalence point is far outside its range.

(b) pH at 20 mL NaOH — buffer region
Moles CH₃COOH initial = 0.1 × 0.025 = 2.5 mmol Moles NaOH added = 0.1 × 0.020 = 2.0 mmol After reaction: CH₃COOH remaining = 2.5 − 2.0 = 0.5 mmol CH₃COO⁻ formed = 2.0 mmol (Volume = 45 mL, but concentrations cancel in H-H equation) pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) = 4.74 + log(2.0/0.5) = 4.74 + log(4) = 4.74 + 0.602 = 5.34
(c) Sharpness of endpoint — dpH/dV
At 24.9 mL NaOH (just before equivalence): Remaining CH₃COOH = 0.1 mmol; CH₃COO⁻ = 2.4 mmol pH = 4.74 + log(2.4/0.1) = 4.74 + 1.38 = 6.12 Volume = 49.9 mL At 25.1 mL NaOH (just after equivalence): Excess OH⁻ = 0.01 mmol in 50.1 mL [OH⁻] = 0.01/50.1 × 1000 = 1.996×10⁻⁴ M pOH = 3.70 → pH = 10.30 ΔpH = 10.30 − 6.12 = 4.18 per 0.2 mL = 20.9 pH units/mL This very steep gradient is why the indicator changes sharply.
