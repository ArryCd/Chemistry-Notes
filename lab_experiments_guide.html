Aim
To determine the amount of Iron(II) in Mohr's salt solution by titration against a standard potassium dichromate (K₂Cr₂O₇) solution using Barium Diphenylamine Sulphonate (BDS) as redox indicator.
Theory — why dichromate?

Dichromate is used as the oxidising titrant instead of permanganate for this experiment. Here's why it is often preferred:

PropertyK₂Cr₂O₇KMnO₄
Primary standard?Yes — stable indefinitely in solution, high purityNo — secondary standard, changes strength over time
Needs indicator?Yes (BDS)No — self-indicator (purple → colourless)
E° value1.33 V (weaker oxidiser)1.51 V (stronger oxidiser)
Works in HCl?Yes — does not oxidise Cl⁻No — oxidises Cl⁻, cannot use HCl
Key reactions
Half reaction — dichromate (oxidant, in burette)
Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 14H⁺ + 6e⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O E° = +1.33 V
Half reaction — iron(II) (reductant, in flask)
Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ + e⁻ E° = −0.77 V
Overall balanced reaction (×6 for iron)
Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 6Fe²⁺ + 14H⁺ → 2Cr³⁺ + 6Fe³⁺ + 7H₂O E°cell = 1.33 − (−0.77) = +0.56 V (spontaneous ✓) Product: Cr³⁺ gives a light greenish colour
BDS indicator — how it works

BDS (Barium Diphenylamine Sulphonate) is a redox indicator — it has two different colours in its oxidised and reduced forms.

Reduced form: colourless (diphenylamine sulphonate) ↓ [O] — oxidation by excess Cr₂O₇²⁻ after all Fe²⁺ is consumed Oxidised form: blue-violet (diphenylbenzidine violet sulphonate)
BDS changes colour only when all Fe²⁺ is consumed and excess Cr₂O₇²⁻ is present to oxidise the indicator itself. So the first permanent blue-violet = endpoint.
The barium salt is used instead of diphenylamine itself because it has better solubility in water — the Ba²⁺ keeps the sulfonate salt dissolved.
Why H₃PO₄ is added — the most important reasoning point

This is a classic exam question. Without H₃PO₄, the endpoint is difficult to detect. Here's the exact reasoning:

1
Problem: Fe³⁺ is yellow
As Fe²⁺ is oxidised → Fe³⁺ during titration, the yellow colour of Fe³⁺ builds up. The blue-violet of BDS is masked by this yellow — you can't see the colour change at the endpoint.
2
Solution: add H₃PO₄ before titration
H₃PO₄ complexes Fe³⁺ → forms [Fe(HPO₄)(H₂O)₅]⁺ — a colourless complex. The yellow Fe³⁺ is removed from solution visually.
Fe³⁺ + H₂PO₄⁻ → [FeIII(HPO₄)(H₂O)₅]⁺ (colourless)
3
Bonus: lowers Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ potential
Complexing Fe³⁺ stabilises it → lowers the standard potential of Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ below 0.77 V. This sharpens the endpoint because BDS (E° ≈ 0.78 V) now changes colour at a potential just above the adjusted Fe couple — the transition is sharp and clear.
If Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ potential is not lowered, both the iron couple and BDS change at similar potentials → sluggish, indistinct endpoint.
Preparation of N/10 K₂Cr₂O₇ standard solution (250 mL)
1
Calculate the mass needed
M(K₂Cr₂O₇) = 294.18 g/mol. n-factor for Cr₂O₇²⁻ = 6 (each Cr goes from +6 to +3, ×2 Cr atoms). Equivalent mass = 294.18/6 = 49.03 g/equiv. For N/10 in 250 mL: mass = (49.03/10) × (250/1000) = 1.225 g
Equivalent mass of K₂Cr₂O₇ = M/6 = 294.18/6 = 49.03 g/equiv Mass for N/10, 250 mL = 49.03 × (1/10) × (250/1000) = 1.225 g
2
Weigh accurately on digital balance
Weigh ~1.225 g of pure K₂Cr₂O₇ accurately. K₂Cr₂O₇ is a primary standard so its purity is high and it can be weighed directly without standardisation.
3
Dissolve and transfer to 250 mL volumetric flask
Dissolve completely in a beaker with a little distilled water. Transfer quantitatively via a funnel into the 250 mL volumetric flask. Rinse the beaker and funnel 2–3 times to ensure complete transfer.
Any material left behind would make the solution weaker than intended — the calculation assumes all 1.225 g is in solution.
4
Make up to 250 mL mark with distilled water
Add distilled water dropwise using a dropper near the mark. The bottom of the meniscus must sit on the 250 mL line when viewed at eye level.
5
Stopper and invert 15–20 times
Invert the stoppered flask repeatedly to ensure the solution is completely homogeneous. The concentration must be uniform throughout the volume.
Titration procedure — step by step
1
Pipette 25 mL of Mohr's salt solution into 250 mL conical flask
Use a clean, rinsed pipette. Mohr's salt = (NH₄)₂SO₄·FeSO₄·6H₂O. This is the source of Fe²⁺ to be estimated.
2
Dilute to 150 mL with distilled water
Add ~125 mL distilled water. Dilution doesn't affect the moles of Fe²⁺ — it just gives a bigger volume to observe colour changes in.
A larger volume makes the colour change at the endpoint easier to see clearly.
3
Add 25 mL of 4N H₂SO₄
Acidic medium is essential — the dichromate half-reaction requires 14H⁺. Without acid, the reaction doesn't proceed and no Cr³⁺ (green) forms. H₂SO₄ is used, not HCl (HCl would be oxidised by Cr₂O₇²⁻ — though less of an issue here than with KMnO₄).
4
Add 5 mL syrupy H₃PO₄
This complexes Fe³⁺ as it forms during titration → removes yellow colour masking and lowers Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ potential → sharp endpoint with BDS.
5
Add 5 drops of BDS indicator
BDS is colourless in reduced state. Very small amount used so it doesn't consume significant titrant.
6
Titrate with standard K₂Cr₂O₇ from burette with constant swirling
Run K₂Cr₂O₇ slowly with constant shaking (swirling). The colour slowly changes from green (Cr³⁺ forming) toward blue-violet at endpoint.
Constant shaking is essential — the reaction between Fe²⁺ and Cr₂O₇²⁻ is not instantaneous near the endpoint. Sluggish mixing can give a false early endpoint.
7
Endpoint: first permanent blue-violet colour
Stop at the first permanent blue-violet colour that does not disappear on swirling. Record burette reading. Repeat for concordant readings (within ±0.1 mL).
Calculation
Key equivalence relationship
1000 mL of 1N K₂Cr₂O₇ ≡ 55.847 g of Fe(II) ≡ 392.143 g of Mohr's salt General: N₁V₁ = N₂V₂ (at equivalence point) N(K₂Cr₂O₇) × V(K₂Cr₂O₇) = N(Fe²⁺) × V(Fe²⁺) If V mL of N/10 K₂Cr₂O₇ is used: moles of Fe²⁺ = (N/10 × V)/1000 equivalents mass of Fe(II) = equivalents × 55.847 g/equiv
The n-factor for Fe²⁺ is 1 (loses 1 electron). For Cr₂O₇²⁻ it is 6 (total 6 electrons gained — 3 per Cr × 2 Cr). This is why the equivalence relationship works out to 1:1 in equivalents despite 6:1 in moles.
Aim
To determine the total hardness of a water sample by complexometric titration with standard EDTA solution using Eriochrome Black T (EBT) as indicator, and express the result as ppm of CaCO₃.
Theory — what is water hardness?

Hardness of water is caused by dissolved Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions (from bicarbonate, chloride, and sulfate salts). These ions form insoluble precipitates with soap, interfere with industrial processes, and cause scale in boilers.

TypeCauseRemoved by
Temporary hardnessCa(HCO₃)₂, Mg(HCO₃)₂Boiling (CO₂ driven off → CaCO₃ precipitates)
Permanent hardnessCaSO₄, MgSO₄, CaCl₂EDTA titration, ion exchange, Na₂CO₃
Total hardnessAll Ca²⁺ + Mg²⁺Measured by EDTA in this experiment

Hardness is expressed as mg/L (ppm) of CaCO₃ equivalent — this is because CaCO₃ (M = 100 g/mol) is a convenient reference; both Ca and Mg are expressed as if they were CaCO₃.

EDTA — why it works so well

EDTA (H₄Y, or H₄EDTA) is a hexadentate ligand — it wraps around metal ions using 4 oxygen atoms from carboxylate groups and 2 nitrogen atoms simultaneously, forming a very stable cage-like (chelate) complex.

M^n+ + Y⁴⁻ → [MY]^(n-4)+ EDTA forms 1:1 complexes with virtually ALL metal ions Very large formation constants (log K_f often 8–18)
The reaction must be done at pH 7–11 (using NH₃/NH₄Cl buffer) so that Y⁴⁻ (the reactive fully deprotonated form) is available in sufficient concentration. At low pH, Y⁴⁻ gets protonated → reacts less effectively.
EBT indicator — the heart of this experiment

EBT (Eriochrome Black T, also called Solochrome Black T) is a metallochromic indicator — it changes colour depending on whether it is free or complexed with a metal ion.

Free EBT (HD²⁻) at pH 7–11 → blue colour M²⁺ + HD²⁻ (blue) → M-D⁻ (wine-red) + H⁺ metal-indicator complex
Stability order (from your PDF): Ca-EDTA > Mg-EDTA > Mg-EBT > Ca-EBT. This order drives the entire mechanism of the titration!

The titration sequence as EDTA is added dropwise:

1
Before titration: solution is wine-red
All Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ are bound to EBT indicator → wine-red M-EBT complexes throughout the solution.
2
During titration: EDTA first reacts with free Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺
Since Ca-EDTA and Mg-EDTA are more stable than Ca-EBT and Mg-EBT, EDTA preferentially binds free metal ions first. Colour remains wine-red (EBT still bound to metals).
3
Near endpoint: EDTA starts displacing EBT from metal complexes
EDTA attacks Mg-EBT (the weakest M-EBT complex). Since Mg-EDTA > Mg-EBT in stability, EDTA displaces EBT: Mg-EBT (wine-red) + EDTA → Mg-EDTA (colourless) + EBT (blue)
4
Endpoint: complete colour change from wine-red → clear blue
All metal ions are now held by EDTA. All EBT is free → clear blue colour. This is the endpoint. Record burette reading.
The endpoint is wine-red → blue, NOT wine-red → colourless. The blue comes from free EBT, not from the absence of any colour.
Step-by-step procedure
1
Pipette 25 mL of hard water into 250 mL conical flask
The water sample contains unknown amounts of Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺. Using exactly 25 mL ensures reproducibility and simplifies calculation.
2
Add 25 mL deionised water
Increases total volume → easier to observe colour change. Deionised water must be used — tap water would add more Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ and give false results.
Never use tap water to dilute — it contains hardness ions and would make your result too high.
3
Add 5 mL NH₃/NH₄Cl buffer solution (pH 7–11)
This maintains pH in the range 7–11 throughout titration. At this pH, EBT exists as HD²⁻ (blue free form) and EDTA as Y⁴⁻ (reactive form). Below pH 7 → EDTA gets protonated, reacts poorly. Above pH 11 → Mg(OH)₂ precipitates, Mg²⁺ not available for EDTA.
The buffer is not optional — without it, the pH would change as H⁺ is released (from M²⁺ + HD²⁻ → MD⁻ + H⁺) and the indicator would stop working at the wrong pH range.
4
Add 30–40 mg EBT indicator (with KNO₃)
EBT is added as a solid mixture with KNO₃ (to aid even distribution and prevent clumping). Solution turns wine-red confirming M-EBT complexes have formed. KNO₃ is inert — just a diluent to help measure out tiny amounts of EBT accurately.
5
Titrate with standard EDTA from burette until wine-red → clear blue
Add EDTA slowly with swirling. The wine-red gradually lightens (purplish) until the sharp, clear blue endpoint. Record V (burette reading).
The last drop at the endpoint should give a clean blue with no reddish tinge. If the blue appears dull or greyish, the endpoint may have been missed — repeat.
6
Repeat for concordant readings
Repeat titration until two readings agree within ±0.1 mL. Take the average V. Concordant results confirm accuracy.
Calculation — total hardness in ppm
Step-by-step derivation
Given: 1000 mL of (M/100) EDTA ≡ 1 g CaCO₃ [since M(CaCO₃) = 100] V mL of f(M/100) EDTA ≡ (1 × V × f)/1000 g CaCO₃ = V × f × 10⁻³ g CaCO₃ where f = strength factor of M/100 EDTA Since 25 mL hard water ≡ V mL EDTA: 25 parts water contain V×f×10⁻³ parts CaCO₃ Scaling to 10⁶ parts water (ppm): 10⁶ parts water contain (V×f×10⁻³ × 10⁶)/25 = V×f×40 parts CaCO₃ ∴ Total hardness = V × f × 40 ppm
The factor 40 = 10⁶/(25 × 10³) comes directly from the volume used (25 mL) and unit conversion. For f = 1 (perfectly made EDTA), hardness = 40V ppm. Memorise: Total hardness = V × f × 40 ppm.

These are the kinds of questions your examiner is most likely to ask. Try answering before revealing.

Experiment 1 — Mohr's salt & dichromate
1. Why is K₂Cr₂O₇ preferred over KMnO₄ for estimating iron(II) in Mohr's salt? very likely
K₂Cr₂O₇ is a primary standard — it can be obtained in high purity and its solution is stable indefinitely. KMnO₄ is a secondary standard whose concentration changes over time due to decomposition. Additionally, K₂Cr₂O₇ does not oxidise Cl⁻, so HCl can be used in the medium if needed. The tradeoff is that K₂Cr₂O₇ (E° = 1.33V) is a weaker oxidiser than KMnO₄ (1.51V), and requires an external indicator (BDS).
2. What is the role of H₃PO₄ in the dichromate titration of iron? very likely
H₃PO₄ serves two purposes: (1) It complexes Fe³⁺ as it forms during titration, producing the colourless [Fe(HPO₄)(H₂O)₅]⁺ complex. This removes the yellow colour of Fe³⁺ which would otherwise mask the blue-violet colour of the BDS indicator at the endpoint. (2) Complexing Fe³⁺ stabilises it and lowers the standard reduction potential of the Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ couple below 0.77 V, creating a sharper and more easily detectable colour change at the endpoint.
3. Explain the mechanism of the BDS indicator in this titration. very likely
BDS (Barium Diphenylamine Sulphonate) is a redox indicator. In its reduced form it is colourless. During titration, all Fe²⁺ is oxidised to Fe³⁺ by Cr₂O₇²⁻. Once all Fe²⁺ is consumed, the first excess drop of Cr₂O₇²⁻ oxidises the BDS indicator itself — irreversibly oxidising it first to the colourless diphenylbenzidine sulphonate and then to the blue-violet diphenylbenzidine violet sulphonate. This colour change (green solution → blue-violet) marks the endpoint. E° of BDS ≈ 0.78 V, which sits just between Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ (after H₃PO₄ addition, ~0.77 V dropped) and Cr₂O₇²⁻/Cr³⁺ (1.33 V).
4. Write the balanced equation for the dichromate-iron reaction and calculate the n-factor for each species. calculation
Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 6Fe²⁺ + 14H⁺ → 2Cr³⁺ + 6Fe³⁺ + 7H₂O. n-factor for Cr₂O₇²⁻: each Cr goes from +6 to +3 = 3e⁻ per Cr, and there are 2 Cr atoms → n-factor = 6. n-factor for Fe²⁺: each Fe goes from +2 to +3 = 1e⁻ → n-factor = 1. At equivalence: N₁V₁ = N₂V₂. The equivalent mass of K₂Cr₂O₇ = 294.18/6 = 49.03 g/equiv.
5. Why must constant swirling be maintained during the titration? reasoning
The reaction between Fe²⁺ and Cr₂O₇²⁻ can be slow near the endpoint, especially as Fe²⁺ concentration decreases. Without constant swirling, locally high concentrations of Cr₂O₇²⁻ at the drop entry point can prematurely oxidise the BDS indicator, giving a false early endpoint. Constant mixing ensures the reagents are uniformly distributed and the true endpoint is detected.
6. Why is H₂SO₄ added, and can HCl be used instead? reasoning
H₂SO₄ is added to provide the acidic medium needed for the Cr₂O₇²⁻ half-reaction (which requires 14H⁺). It also prevents hydrolysis of Cr³⁺. HCl is generally avoided with KMnO₄ (which would oxidise Cl⁻ to Cl₂, using up permanganate — the Zimmermann-Reinhardt problem). With K₂Cr₂O₇, HCl oxidation is less of a concern since E°(Cr₂O₇²⁻/Cr³⁺) = 1.33 V < E°(Cl₂/Cl⁻) = 1.36 V, making the reaction thermodynamically unfavoured. However, H₂SO₄ is still preferred for cleanliness.
Experiment 2 — Hardness of water
7. What is meant by total hardness of water and how is it expressed? very likely
Total hardness is the total concentration of Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions in water, expressed as the equivalent amount of CaCO₃ in mg per litre (ppm). CaCO₃ is used as the reference standard because of its convenient molar mass (100 g/mol) and historical convention. Both Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ are converted to their CaCO₃ equivalents for expression. Total hardness = temporary (removable by boiling) + permanent hardness.
8. Explain the role of the NH₃/NH₄Cl buffer in this titration. very likely
The NH₃/NH₄Cl buffer maintains pH in the range 7–11, which is essential for two reasons: (1) At this pH, EDTA exists predominantly as Y⁴⁻ (the fully deprotonated, reactive form) in sufficient concentration to react completely with Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺. At lower pH, EDTA is protonated and reacts incompletely. (2) EBT indicator functions correctly as HD²⁻ (blue free form) only in this pH range. If pH exceeds ~11, Mg(OH)₂ precipitates and Mg²⁺ is unavailable for titration, giving falsely low results.
9. Explain why the stability order Ca-EDTA > Mg-EDTA > Mg-EBT > Ca-EBT governs the endpoint mechanism. conceptual
At the start, both Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ are bound to EBT (wine-red). As EDTA is added, it first reacts with free Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ in solution (since Ca-EDTA and Mg-EDTA are more stable products). Near the endpoint, EDTA starts attacking Mg-EBT (weakest M-EBT complex), displacing EBT and forming the more stable Mg-EDTA. Finally EDTA also displaces EBT from Ca-EBT. All EBT is freed → blue. The driving force is always towards the formation of the most stable complex (EDTA complexes). Without this stability order, the displacement couldn't occur and no colour change would be seen.
10. Derive the formula: Total hardness = V × f × 40 ppm. derivation
Start: 1000 mL of M/100 EDTA ≡ 1 g CaCO₃ (since M(CaCO₃)=100, 1 mole EDTA = 1 mole CaCO₃). So V mL of f(M/100) EDTA ≡ V×f/1000 g CaCO₃ = V×f×10⁻³ g CaCO₃. This came from 25 mL of hard water. Hardness in ppm = g CaCO₃ per 10⁶ g water. Since density of water ≈ 1, 25 mL ≈ 25 g. So 25 g water contains V×f×10⁻³ g CaCO₃. Scaling to 10⁶ g: hardness = (V×f×10⁻³/25) × 10⁶ = V×f×40 ppm.
11. Why must deionised water be used for dilution — not tap water? practical
Tap water itself contains Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions (it has hardness). Adding tap water to dilute the sample would add extra Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ to the conical flask. These would consume additional EDTA during titration, giving a burette reading V that is too high → calculated hardness would be erroneously high. Deionised water contains no hardness ions so it doesn't interfere with the measurement.
12. Why is EBT used with KNO₃, and why is only 30–40 mg used? practical
EBT is used as a solid mixture with KNO₃ (usually 1:100 ratio) because EBT is needed in very small amounts and it's difficult to weigh out accurately at 1–2 mg scale. Diluting with inert KNO₃ allows a 30–40 mg portion to be weighed reliably while delivering the right amount of indicator. KNO₃ is chemically inert and does not interfere. Using too much EBT would make the solution too intensely wine-red and the endpoint colour change would be unclear; too little and the initial colour would be too faint to observe.
