Werner's theory — primary vs secondary valencycore concept
Three types of bonding
  • Ionic — electrostatic interaction between anion & cation; complete separation of electrons; complete transfer
  • Covalent — not a transfer; 2 electrons shared, 1 by each atom
  • Coordinate — one atom donates, not completely; donation of lone pair
K[Au(CN)₂] — worked example

Potassium aurocyanate K[Au(CN)₂] is used in gold-plating as an electrolyte. Here Au → +1 (primary valency). This is a coordination compound where CN⁻ are ligands sharing electrons with Au⁺.

Primary vs secondary valency — Werner's postulates
[Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃
Primary valency = 3 (Co³⁺ charge, satisfied by 3 Cl⁻ outside)
Secondary valency = 6 (6 NH₃ inside, sharing electrons)
With AgNO₃ → 3 AgCl (3 free Cl⁻ outside)
Conductivity HIGH — 4 total ions present
[Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl
Primary valency = 3 (only 1 free Cl⁻ outside)
Secondary valency = 6 (4 NH₃ + 2 Cl inside)
With AgNO₃ → only 1 AgCl
Inner Cl acts as ligand AND charge balance simultaneously
Conductivity is highest in [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃ (4 ions) because more free ions outside the coordination sphere means higher conductivity. [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl has only 2 free ions.
Werner's geometric proof — why octahedral?PM Sirimportant

For MA₄B₂ type compounds, Werner tried to find how many isomers of [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂] exist. He considered three possible geometries:

GeometryPredicted isomers for MA₄B₂Result
Planar (metal in centre)3 arrangements possibleToo many
Trigonal prism3 arrangementsToo many
OctahedralOnly 2 (cis and trans)✓ Matches experiment

Werner was able to isolate only 2 isomers chemically, so he concluded the structure must be octahedral. This was the decisive experimental proof.

Mohn salt, alum, and similar compounds (non-coordination compounds) can take many forms. But coordination compounds with only 2 isomers → octahedral structure is the only explanation.
Second compound type: MA₃B₃

For [Co(NH₃)₃Cl₃] type: octahedral gives exactly 2 isomers (fac and mer). This further confirmed the octahedral geometry.

Why coordination compounds form — key insight

In a free Co²⁺ ion, all 5 d-orbitals are degenerate (same energy). All 7 electrons have same energy — only possible if no ligands have come. When a ligand approaches along an axis, it repels electrons in orbitals along that axis more than others → d-orbital splitting → basis of CFT.

PM Sir's insight: the 4th electron in t₂g in a complex needs energy to either pair (pairing energy favorable) or go to the higher eg state. Which is cheaper decides high spin vs low spin. This is the decision point.
d-orbital shapes and the basis of CFTPM Sirfoundation

The 5 d-orbitals have distinct spatial orientations. In a free ion (no ligands), all 5 are degenerate. When ligands approach, orbitals pointing directly at ligands experience more repulsion.

OrbitalPoints alongIn octahedral field
dz²z-axis (lobes on z)eₘ set — high energy (+0.6Δₒ)
dx²-y²x and y axeseₘ set — high energy (+0.6Δₒ)
dxybetween x and y axest₂g set — low energy (−0.4Δₒ)
dyzbetween y and z axest₂g set — low energy (−0.4Δₒ)
dxzbetween x and z axest₂g set — low energy (−0.4Δₒ)
In octahedral complexes, ligands approach along ±x, ±y, ±z axes. The eₘ orbitals (dz², dx²-y²) point directly at ligands → maximum repulsion → higher energy. The t₂g orbitals point between ligands → less repulsion → lower energy.
Octahedral splitting — energy levels, Δₒ and 10Dqcore
Octahedral field splitting: eₘ (dz², dx²-y²) → +0.6Δₒ = +6Dq (higher energy) t₂g (dxy, dyz, dxz) → −0.4Δₒ = −4Dq (lower energy) Total gap = Δₒ = 10Dq Note: Dq is an older notation, 10Dq = Δₒ = the energy gap
Tetrahedral splitting — inverted
Tetrahedral field (ligands approach between axes, not along them): t₂ (dxy, dyz, dxz) → higher energy (+4Dq_tet) e (dz², dx²-y²) → lower energy (−6Dq_tet) Key relationship: (10Dq)tet = (4/9)(10Dq)oct = Δt = (4/9)Δₒ
Square planar splitting — from PM Sir notes
Splitting order (bottom to top): dz² (lowest) dyz, dxz dxy dx²-y² (highest — points directly at 4 ligands in xy plane)
Square planar has a much larger energy gap for dx²-y² than octahedral, because removing the two z-axis ligands pushes the xy-plane ligands closer in → dx²-y² shoots up in energy.
Factors affecting Δₒ (CFSE magnitude)PM Sirimportant
1. Strength of ligand (spectrochemical series)
Weak field: Cl⁻, F⁻, Br⁻, I⁻, water → small Δₒ → high spin Moderate field: NH₃ → intermediate Strong field: CO, CN⁻ → large Δₒ → low spin
2. Charge of metal ion/cation

Higher charge → metal pulls ligands closer → stronger ligand field → larger Δₒ. Example from PM Sir notes: [V(H₂O)₆]³⁺ > [V(H₂O)₆]²⁺ in CFSE because 3+ pulls water ligands closer than 2+.

3. Nature of metal ion — period effect
Splitting increases going down a group (same charge): 3d < 4d < 5d Example: Co³⁺ < Rh³⁺ < Ir³⁺ Heavier metals have more diffuse d-orbitals → stronger interaction with ligands
Size factor vs CFSE factor — Ni²⁺ geometry choice

According to CFSE alone, Ni²⁺ should always prefer octahedral. But with Cl⁻ (large, weak-field ligand), the size factor dominates — Ni²⁺ chooses the geometry that fits the smaller Cl⁻ ligand, which is tetrahedral. With CN⁻ (CFSE dominates) → square planar.

CN⁻Square planar [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻Strong field → CFSE dominates → sq. planar
High spin vs low spin — the 4th electron decisionPM Sirkey concept

For d⁴ and above, the 4th electron has a choice: pair in t₂g (costs pairing energy P) or go to eg (costs Δₒ). Which is cheaper determines spin state.

If Δₒ > P → pairing is cheaper → LOW SPIN (strong field) If Δₒ < P → going to eg is cheaper → HIGH SPIN (weak field) For tetrahedral: Δt = (4/9)Δₒ, so Δt << P almost always → tetrahedral complexes are almost always high spin
This is why tetrahedral complexes are rarely low spin — the splitting is just too small to overcome pairing energy.
High spin configurations (your notes use these often)
d⁵ high spin: t₂g³ eg² (all singly occupied — 5 unpaired) d⁶ high spin: t₂g⁴ eg² (4 unpaired) d⁷ high spin: t₂g⁵ eg² (3 unpaired)
CFSE formula and general methodmethod
CFSE = (n_t₂g × −4Dq) + (n_eg × +6Dq) + extra pairing energy Or in Δₒ units: CFSE = (n_t₂g × −0.4Δₒ) + (n_eg × +0.6Δₒ) + ΔP Where ΔP = (extra forced pairs vs free ion) × P
Only add pairing energy for pairs that are EXTRA compared to the free ion. If the free ion already has 2 pairs (like d⁶ high spin → 1 pair; d⁶ low spin → 3 pairs → 2 extra pairs), then ΔP = 2P.
CFSE for Mn²⁺ low spin octahedralworked
Mn²⁺ → d⁵ Low spin: t₂g⁵ eg⁰ CFSE = 5×(−4Dq) + 0×(+6Dq) = −20Dq = −2.0Δₒ + pairing energy correction: +2P (high spin d⁵ has zero pairs; low spin has 2 extra pairs → add 2P) Final CFSE = −20Dq + 2P
CFSE for Co²⁺ (d⁷) — high spin and low spinworked
High spin Co²⁺ (from PM Sir notes)
d⁷ high spin: t₂g⁵ eg² CFSE = 5×(−4) + 2×(+6) = −20 + 12 = −8Dq (no pairing correction needed — HS d⁷ already has 2 paired electrons in t₂g, same as free ion)
Low spin Co²⁺
d⁷ low spin: t₂g⁶ eg¹ → 3 pairs in t₂g CFSE = 6×(−4) + 1×(+6) = −24 + 6 = −18Dq Co²⁺ free ion (HS) already has 2 paired electrons. Low spin has 3 pairs → 1 extra pair forced Correction: +1P (not +2P — only 1 extra pair) Final CFSE = −18Dq + P
PM Sir's note: Co²⁺ already freely has 2 pairing electrons. Low spin t₂g⁶ has 3 pairs. Extra pairs = 3−2 = 1. So add only 1P, not 2P.
CFSE for Ni²⁺ (d⁸) — octahedral vs tetrahedralworked
Octahedral Ni²⁺
d⁸ octahedral: t₂g⁶ eg² CFSE = 6×(−4Dq) + 2×(+6Dq) = −24 + 12 = −12Dq_oct
Tetrahedral Ni²⁺ (inverted splitting: e and t₂)
d⁸ tetrahedral: e⁴ t₂⁴ (lower set e filled first in tet) CFSE = 4×(−6Dq_tet) + 4×(+4Dq_tet) = −24Dq_tet + 16Dq_tet = −8Dq_tet Using (Dq_tet) = (4/9)(Dq_oct): = −8 × (4/9)Dq_oct = −(32/9)Dq_oct ≈ −4Dq_oct (approximate) So: CFSE(oct) = −12Dq vs CFSE(tet) ≈ −4Dq Ni²⁺ gains 3× more stabilisation in octahedral → prefers octahedral!
This is the CFSE preference argument for spinel site preference. Ni²⁺ has a strong site preference for octahedral holes — CFSE difference = 12−4 = 8Dq_oct per Ni²⁺.
CFSE for Fe³⁺ (d⁵) — why it has no preferenceworked
Fe³⁺ → d⁵ high spin (O²⁻ is weak field ligand in oxides) Octahedral: t₂g³ eg² CFSE = 3×(−4) + 2×(+6) = −12 + 12 = 0 Dq Tetrahedral: e² t₂³ CFSE = 2×(−6) + 3×(+4) = −12 + 12 = 0 Dq Fe³⁺ CFSE = 0 in BOTH sites → NO site preference → Fe³⁺ will not decide the nature of a spinel
This is why Fe²⁺ (not Fe³⁺) decides whether Fe₃O₄ is normal or inverse spinel — Fe³⁺ has zero CFSE preference and is indifferent to which site it occupies.
Spinel structure AB₂O₄ — crystal structure basicsPM Sirimportant

Spinel formula: AB₂O₄, where A is A²⁺ cation and B is B³⁺ cation. O²⁻ is a weak-field ligand → all complexes are high spin.

Crystal structure
  • Oxide ions form FCC lattice (CCP — cubic close packing)
  • For each unit cell (containing 4 O²⁻ ions): 8 tetrahedral holes and 4 octahedral holes available
  • eg level → e⁻ repulsion (size increases with eg filling)
  • t₂g level → no e⁻ repulsion (orbitals point away from ligands)
Normal spinel
A²⁺ → tetrahedral holes
2 B³⁺ → octahedral holes
e.g. MnFe₂O₄ (Mn²⁺ in tet, 2Fe³⁺ in oct)
Inverse spinel
A²⁺ → octahedral holes
1 B³⁺ → tetrahedral holes
1 B³⁺ → octahedral holes
e.g. Fe₃O₄
Three spinel examples — full reasoningPM Sir + your notesexam favourite
1. Mn₃O₄ = Mn²⁺Mn₂³⁺O₄ → Normal spinel
Mn³⁺ → d⁴ system Octahedral CFSE: t₂g³ eg¹ → 3×(−4) + 1×(+6) = −6Dq_oct Tetrahedral: e² t₂² → 2×(−6) + 2×(+4) = −4Dq_tet ≈ −1.8Dq_oct Octahedral CFSE is more → Mn³⁺ prefers oct → Normal spinel
2. Fe₃O₄ = Fe²⁺Fe₂³⁺O₄ → Inverse spinel
Fe³⁺ (d⁵ HS): CFSE = 0 in both oct and tet → NO preference, Fe³⁺ won't decide Fe²⁺ (d⁶ HS): t₂g⁴ eg² → CFSE in oct = 4×(−4)+2×(6) = −4Dq_oct CFSE in tet ≈ −(4/9)×same ≈ smaller Fe²⁺ prefers octahedral → takes oct holes → Fe³⁺ forced to split (one tet, one oct) → INVERSE SPINEL
3. Co₃O₄ = Co²⁺Co₂³⁺O₄ → Normal spinel (exception!)
By CFSE calculation alone, Co₃O₄ should be inverse spinel. BUT: Co³⁺ (d⁶) almost ALWAYS forms low-spin complexes → CFSE = −18Dq_oct (very large) Co³⁺ only forms high spin in TWO exceptions: [CoF₆]³⁻ and [CoF₃(H₂O)₃] (F⁻ is the weakest ligand) Co³⁺ has a very strong preference for octahedral (low spin) → takes all oct holes → NORMAL SPINEL (experiment confirms)
Remember Co₃O₄ as the exception: calculation says inverse, reality is normal, because Co³⁺ is almost always low spin with its exceptionally large CFSE.
NiFe₂O₄ — inverse spinel CFSE proofworked
NiFe₂O₄: Ni²⁺ and 2Fe³⁺ Fe³⁺ (d⁵): CFSE = 0 everywhere → no preference Ni²⁺ (d⁸): Oct CFSE = −12Dq_oct Tet CFSE = −8Dq_tet ≈ −4Dq_oct Difference = 8Dq_oct (strong preference for oct) Ni²⁺ goes to octahedral holes. 1 Fe³⁺ → tetrahedral, 1 Fe³⁺ → octahedral → INVERSE SPINEL (confirmed)
Ionic radius trend across 3d series — CFT applicationPM Sirapplication

PM Sir drew the ionic radius vs 3d metal ion graph (aqua complexes). The curve is a double-humped shape, not a smooth decrease. This is directly due to CFT:

  • Filling t₂g orbitals (which point away from ligands) → does NOT cause extra repulsion → metal-ligand bond can shorten → radius decreases
  • Filling eg orbitals (which point at ligands) → causes direct repulsion → pushes ligands away → effective radius increases at Ca and Zn-like positions
  • The double hump appears at d⁵ (Mn²⁺) where both sets are half-filled symmetrically, and at d¹⁰ (Zn²⁺) where no CFSE exists
3d series radii trend for M²⁺ aqua complexes: Ti²⁺ → V²⁺ → Cr²⁺ → Mn²⁺ → Fe²⁺ → Co²⁺ → Ni²⁺ → Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ (double-humped decrease, not smooth) eg filling → e⁻ repulsion → size increases t₂g filling → no e⁻ repulsion → size decreases (CFSE stabilisation)
ZnO → initially white colour, turns yellow on heating because: ZnO → Zn²⁺ + O²⁻, but after heating O²⁻ count < Zn²⁺ (metal excess defect). Liberated electrons create colour by electronic transitions.
CoSO₄ in water and [CoCl₄]²⁻ — colour contrastPM Sirkey example
[Co(H₂O)₆]SO₄ — pale pink
CoSO₄ dissolved in water → [Co(H₂O)₆]SO₄ → pale pink colour Co²⁺ → d⁷ system → octahedral (6 water ligands) Configuration: t₂g⁵ eg² Electronic transition: t₂g → eg (uses visible light to cross energy gap) Energy gap is sized so that COMPLEMENTARY colour is pink → observed as pale pink Why PALE? [CoCl₄]²⁻ — dark blue (intense!)
Co²⁺ → d⁷ → tetrahedral with 4 Cl⁻ Configuration: e⁴ t₂³ (inverted tet splitting) Tetrahedral → NO centre of symmetry → d-p orbitals fully mixed → Laporte selection rule RELAXED → p→p, d→p, p→d transitions all occur (Δl = ±1 allowed) Energy gap → complementary colour is blue → DARK BLUE (intense) Why DARK? Laporte relaxed → allowed transitions → high intensity
Pale vs dark depends on INTENSITY = number of photons absorbed = number of electronic transitions. Tetrahedral complexes are more intensely coloured because Laporte rule is relaxed.
Selection rules for d-d transitionsmust know
Two selection rules
1. Spin selection rule: ΔS = 0 (spin must be conserved) → d-d transitions obey this (electrons don't flip spin) 2. Laporte selection rule: Δl = ±1 → d-d transitions DISOBEY this (d→d means Δl=0, forbidden) → but transitions still appear WEAKLY because: a) Vibronic coupling: molecular vibrations temporarily remove centre of symmetry → transition allowed momentarily b) In tetrahedral: no centre of symmetry → d-p mixing → transitions gain some p-character → effectively allowed

O₂ paramagnetism and haemoglobin — applicationsPM Sirapplication
O₂ paramagnetism
₈O = 1s² 2s² 2p⁴ → paramagnetic (2 unpaired electrons in 2p) ₇N = 1s² 2s² 2p³ → wait... N₂ is diamagnetic despite N being paramagnetic (because N₂ has bonding MOs that pair all electrons)
Fe²⁺ in haemoglobin — full mechanism
Before oxygenation (deoxyhaemoglobin): Fe²⁺ (d⁶ HS): t₂g⁴ eg² → 4 unpaired electrons → PARAMAGNETIC Oxygenation process: Fe²⁺ gives 1e⁻ to O₂ → Fe²⁺ becomes Fe³⁺ O₂ + e⁻ → O₂⁻ (superoxide — 1 unpaired electron) Fe³⁺ (d⁵ LS in porphyrin): t₂g⁵ eg⁰ → 1 unpaired electron After oxygenation (oxyhaemoglobin): Fe³⁺ (1 unpaired) + O₂⁻ (1 unpaired) These two electrons couple ANTIFERROMAGNETICALLY → cancel out → Overall DIAMAGNETIC in oxyhaemoglobin
Deoxygenated haemoglobin = paramagnetic (Fe²⁺, 4 unpaired). Oxygenated haemoglobin = diamagnetic (Fe³⁺ + O₂⁻ coupled). This difference is used in MRI imaging (BOLD contrast).

Dichrometry — estimation of Fe in Mohr's salt (lab)lab experiment
Mohr's Salt: (NH₄)₂SO₄ · FeSO₄ · 6H₂O K₂Cr₂O₇ = PRIMARY standard (stable, doesn't change strength over time) KMnO₄ = SECONDARY standard (changes strength over time — weaker oxidiser adds) Overall reaction: Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 14H⁺ + 6Fe²⁺ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O + 6Fe³⁺ (light greenish endpoint)
Why H₃PO₄ is added (two reasons)
1. Fe³⁺ (yellow) masks BDS endpoint colour H₃PO₄ + Fe³⁺ → [Fe(HPO₄)(H₂O)₅]⁺ (COLOURLESS) → yellow colour removed → endpoint visible 2. Lowers Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ standard potential below 0.77V → creates sharp potential break at endpoint → BDS (E° ≈ 0.78V) changes colour just above Fe couple
BDS indicator mechanism

BDS = Barium Diphenylamine Sulphonate. Before endpoint: colourless (reduced form). Once all Fe²⁺ consumed, excess Cr₂O₇²⁻ oxidises BDS → green → blue-violet (endpoint). Ba salt used for better water solubility vs diphenylamine.

Permanganometry — KMnO₄ reactions and Nernst equationcalculation
MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O E° = 1.51 V (self-indicator) Fe³⁺ + e⁻ → Fe²⁺ E° = 0.77 V Nernst: E = E° + (0.059/n) × log([Ox]/[Red]) E(MnO₄⁻) = 1.51 + (0.059/5) × log([MnO₄⁻][H⁺]⁸/[Mn²⁺]) Comparison: E°(MnO₄⁻/Mn²⁺) = 1.51 V (stronger oxidiser) E°(Cr₂O₇²⁻/Cr³⁺) = 1.33 V E°(Cl₂/Cl⁻) = 1.36 V → K₂Cr₂O₇ cannot oxidise Cl⁻ to Cl₂ (E° too low)
Effect of complex formation on redox potential
H₃PO₄ complexes Fe³⁺ → reduces [Fe³⁺] → shifts Nernst equation If [Fe³⁺] reduced: E = 0.77 + 0.059 × log([Fe³⁺]/[Fe²⁺]) decreases → standard potential of Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ couple drops below 0.77V → endpoint sharper
Estimation of Mn²⁺ using KMnO₄ — indirect methodTKM Sirnew

Mn²⁺ cannot be directly titrated with KMnO₄ (same element). An indirect back-titration method is used.

Step 1: Oxidise Mn²⁺ to MnO₄⁻ using NaBiO₃ + HNO₃ Mn²⁺ + NaBiO₃ + HNO₃ → MnO₄⁻ + excess products Step 2: Add measured excess of Mohr's salt (50 mL) = V₂ (standard Fe²⁺ solution to reduce back the MnO₄⁻) Step 3: Titrate excess Mohr's salt (unreacted Fe²⁺) using standard KMnO₄ = V₁ Volume of KMnO₄ equivalent to Mn²⁺ = V₁ − V₂
This is an example of a back-titration: you overshoot the reaction, then titrate back the excess. The KMnO₄ equivalent to Mn²⁺ = difference in volumes.
Iodometry — estimation of I₂ and Cu²⁺iodometry
Iodimetry: direct titration with standard I₂ solution (analyte = reducing agent) Iodometry: indirect — analyte oxidises I⁻ → I₂; then I₂ titrated with Na₂S₂O₃ (analyte = oxidising agent e.g. Cu²⁺, Cr₂O₇²⁻) I₂ + Na₂S₂O₃: S₂O₃²⁻ + I₂ → S₄O₆²⁻ + I⁻ (indicator: starch → blue-black with I₂) I₂ is partially soluble in water, so add excess KI: I₂ + KI → I₃⁻ (highly water soluble)
Cu²⁺ estimation by iodometry
Cu²⁺ + I⁻ → CuI↓ + I₂ (Cu²⁺ oxidises I⁻) Then: I₂ + Na₂S₂O₃ → titrate with starch indicator Reaction driven by tiny Ksp of CuI (10⁻¹²) keeping [Cu⁺] very low → forward reaction E calculation: E = 0.85 + 0.059 × log([Cu²⁺][I⁻])
EDTA structure, pKa values and metal complexcore

EDTA (H₄EDTA) has 6 donor sites — 4 carboxylate oxygens + 2 nitrogen atoms. Forms very stable 1:1 complexes with almost all metal ions. Basic medium required because Y⁴⁻ is the reactive form.

pKa₁ ≈ 1.99 | pKa₂ ≈ 2.67 | pKa₃ ≈ 6.16 | pKa₄ ≈ 10.26 H₄EDTA ⇌ H₃EDTA⁻ ⇌ H₂EDTA²⁻ ⇌ HEDTA³⁻ ⇌ Y⁴⁻ M^n+ + H₄EDTA ⇌ [M-EDTA]^(n-4)+ + 4H⁺ Overall charge on complex = (n−4)+ Stability constant = product of all stepwise stability constants
Three types of EDTA titrationsTKM Sirexam structure

B. Back titration

Why Ca²⁺-EBT is unstable (and what to do)

Ca-EBT complex is less stable than Mg-EBT. Direct titration of Ca²⁺ with EBT gives no sharp endpoint. Solution: add a small amount of Mg²⁺ to the solution. Mg²⁺ binds EBT (wine-red). Ca²⁺ displaces Mg²⁺ from Mg-EDTA (Ca-EDTA > Mg-EDTA). Released Mg²⁺ binds EBT → gives the wine-red colour → sharp endpoint when EDTA finally displaces EBT from Mg-EBT.

Principles of gravimetric analysisprinciples

Gravimetric analysis is described as the most precise and accurate method. The analyte is converted to a sparingly soluble precipitate, filtered, dried/ignited, and weighed.

Classification of volumetric analysis (from TKM Sir)
Acid-base titration → volumetric / titrimetric analysis Complexometric titration → volumetric analysis Redox titration → volumetric analysis Gravimetric analysis → mass-based, NOT volumetric
Gravimetric factor
GF = Molar mass of analyte / Molar mass of precipitate

Common gravimetric estimations — SO₄²⁻ and Cl⁻worked
Sulphate estimation: BaCl₂ + SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄↓ + 2Cl⁻ Filter, ignite at ~800°C, weigh as BaSO₄ mass SO₄²⁻ = mass BaSO₄ × GF(0.4116) Chloride estimation: AgNO₃ + Cl⁻ → AgCl↓ + NO₃⁻ Dry at 120°C (do NOT ignite — AgCl decomposes in light/heat) mass Cl⁻ = mass AgCl × GF(0.2474)
Ni²⁺ estimation using DMG — full procedure and calculationTKM Sirworked experiment
Reaction
Ni²⁺ + 2 DMG → Ni(DMG)₂↓ (brick red precipitate) M(Ni) = 58.7 g/mol, M(DMG) = 116.12 g/mol M(Ni(DMG)₂) = 58.7 + 2×116.12 = 288.7 g/mol (× g precipitate formed) GF = 58.7 / 288.7 = 0.203
Why slightly alkaline medium
Acidic medium: H⁺ picks up O-H groups → H-bonding disrupted → no precipitate Strongly basic: both O-H hydrogens removed → no intramolecular H-bond → no ppt Slightly alkaline: DMG forms stable intramolecular H-bonds in chelate ring → brick red Ni-DMG ppt
Procedure (from TKM Sir)
1. Ni-DMG precipitate formed in solution 2. Filter through G₄-crucible (sintered glass, not ignition) 3. W₁ = weight of empty crucible 4. Wash precipitate, DRY (do NOT ignite — DMG decomposes on heating) 5. W₂ = weight of crucible + Ni-DMG precipitate 6. Weight of Ni-DMG = (W₂ − W₁) grams = x grams
Calculation (TKM Sir)
Amount of Ni in precipitate = (58.7 × x) / 288.7 grams Amount of Ni in 1 litre of solution = (58.7 × x / 288.7) × 40 (×40 factor comes from 25 mL sample → scale to 1000 mL)
Thermogravimetric analysis — CuSO₄·5H₂OTKM Sirnew

Thermogravimetric method tracks mass loss as temperature increases. For CuSO₄·5H₂O, water molecules leave in stages at different temperatures.

TG curve for CuSO₄·5H₂O (weight vs temperature): Step 1: −18 (weight loss) → 2 H₂O molecules escape Step 2: −18 (weight loss) → 2 more H₂O molecules escape Step 3: −54 → last H₂O + further dehydration products Each plateau on the curve = a stable intermediate compound The curve has 3 distinct weight-loss steps → confirms 5 water molecules in 3 different environments
The thermogravimetric method tells you not just how many water molecules are present, but in how many distinct environments — some waters are more tightly bound than others.
Electrogravimetric methodTKM Sirnew

Electrogravimetric analysis uses electrolysis to deposit the analyte onto an electrode, which is then weighed. The mass difference of the electrode gives the mass of analyte.

Example: Cu²⁺ estimation Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu° (deposited on electrode) Weigh electrode before and after electrolysis Mass of Cu = (final electrode mass − initial electrode mass)

This is distinct from the DMG gravimetric method (chemical precipitation). Electrogravimetry deposits the metal directly as an element, so no gravimetric factor is needed — the deposit IS the analyte.
