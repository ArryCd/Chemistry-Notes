Coordination compounds & Werner's theory
C
Werner isolated only 2 isomers of [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄCl‚āā]Cl from the reaction mixture. Using this experimental fact, explain how Werner ruled out the planar and trigonal prismatic geometries and proved the octahedral structure.
▼
Full explanation

Werner reasoned from the number of isomers backwards. For a compound MA‚āĄB‚āā, the number of geometric isomers depends entirely on the geometry around the central metal:

Planar geometry ‚Üí 3 possible arrangements for MA‚āĄB‚āā Trigonal prism ‚Üí 3 possible arrangements Octahedral ‚Üí only 2 arrangements (cis and trans)

Since Werner could only ever isolate 2 isomers of [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄCl‚āā]Cl experimentally, planar and trigonal prismatic geometries were eliminated ‚ÄĒ they would predict 3 isomers. Only the octahedral geometry accounts for exactly 2.

He further confirmed this with MA‚āÉB‚āÉ compounds (e.g. [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÉCl‚āÉ]): octahedral predicts exactly 2 isomers (facial where all 3 B are on one triangular face, and meridional where 3 B span a meridional plane). Werner isolated exactly 2, confirming octahedral.

Exam tip: always state the predicted isomer count for each geometry, then contrast with the experimental observation. The logic is purely deductive ‚ÄĒ ruling out geometries by elimination.
C
Explain why [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]Cl‚āÉ gives 3 moles of AgCl with AgNO‚āÉ, but [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄCl‚āā]Cl gives only 1 mole. How does this relate to Werner's primary and secondary valency?
▼
Werner's valency theory

Primary valency is the oxidation state of the metal ‚ÄĒ it is ionisable and must be satisfied by anions outside the coordination sphere. Secondary valency is the coordination number ‚ÄĒ it is non-ionisable and must be satisfied by ligands directly bonded inside.

[Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]Cl‚āÉ: Primary valency = 3 ‚Üí 3 Cl‚ĀĽ outside coordination sphere ‚Üí ionisable ‚Üí 3 AgCl with AgNO‚āÉ; total ions in solution = 4 (1 complex cation + 3 Cl‚ĀĽ) [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄCl‚āā]Cl: Primary valency = 3 ‚Üí only 1 Cl‚ĀĽ outside (satisfies 1 primary valency unit) 2 Cl‚ĀĽ are inside coordination sphere as ligands (satisfy secondary valency) ‚Üí only 1 AgCl with AgNO‚āÉ; total ions = 2
The inner Cl‚ĀĽ in [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄCl‚āā]Cl simultaneously acts as a ligand AND as a charge-balance ion ‚ÄĒ this is the key dual role. The outer Cl‚ĀĽ only balances charge and is freely ionisable.

Crystal field theory & CFSE
C
Explain why d‚Äďd transitions in octahedral complexes are "Laporte forbidden" yet still produce colour. Why are tetrahedral complexes more intensely coloured than octahedral?
▼
Selection rules and colour intensity

Laporte rule: transitions between orbitals of the same parity (both gerade: g‚Üíg, or both ungerade: u‚Üíu) are forbidden. In an octahedral field, all 5 d-orbitals are gerade (g). Therefore d‚Üíd means g‚Üíg ‚Üí Laporte forbidden.

Despite this, octahedral complexes still show colour (weakly) because vibronic coupling occurs: molecular vibrations (particularly asymmetric modes) temporarily remove the centre of symmetry. During these brief distortions, the Laporte rule is momentarily relaxed, allowing weak d‚Äďd transitions. This is why octahedral d‚Äďd bands are pale (low molar absorptivity, őĶ ‚Čą 5‚Äď50 L mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ cm‚ĀĽ¬Ļ).

Tetrahedral complexes (e.g. [CoCl‚āĄ]¬≤‚ĀĽ): ‚Üí NO centre of symmetry (T_d point group) ‚Üí d and p orbitals of the SAME symmetry species mix freely ‚Üí d-orbitals gain partial p-character ‚Üí transitions are effectively d‚Üí(d+p) which has őĒl ‚Č† 0 ‚Üí allowed ‚Üí őĶ ‚Čą 200‚Äď1000 L mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ cm‚ĀĽ¬Ļ ‚Üí intense, dark colour [Co(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≤‚Āļ: pale pink (őĶ ‚Čą 5) [CoCl‚āĄ]¬≤‚ĀĽ: dark blue (őĶ ‚Čą 600)
The number of photons absorbed = number of transitions. More allowed transitions ‚Üí more intense absorption ‚Üí darker, richer colour.

Why CN‚ĀĽ ‚Üí square planar but Cl‚ĀĽ ‚Üí tetrahedral

Two competing factors determine geometry:

CFSE factor: Square planar has the largest energy gap (dx¬≤-y¬≤ pushed very high). Strong-field ligands like CN‚ĀĽ create large őĒ‚āí ‚Üí splitting large enough that sq. planar CFSE > tet CFSE. With CN‚ĀĽ, CFSE factor dominates ‚Üí [Ni(CN)‚āĄ]¬≤‚ĀĽ square planar, diamagnetic.

Size factor: Cl‚ĀĽ is a large ligand. Steric repulsion between 4 large Cl‚ĀĽ ligands in square planar (all in same plane, 90¬į apart) is severe. Tetrahedral geometry (109.5¬į) relieves this repulsion. For Cl‚ĀĽ, size factor overrides CFSE ‚Üí [NiCl‚āĄ]¬≤‚ĀĽ tetrahedral, paramagnetic (2 unpaired, őľ = 2.83 BM).

Factors affecting őĒ‚āí

1. Nature of ligand (spectrochemical series): Strong-field ligands (CO, CN‚ĀĽ) cause large splitting; weak-field (Cl‚ĀĽ, Br‚ĀĽ, H‚āāO) cause small splitting. CN‚ĀĽ can ŌÄ-backbond with metal d-orbitals (accepting electron density into ŌÄ* orbitals) ‚Üí enhances splitting dramatically.

2. Charge of the metal ion: Higher oxidation state ‚Üí metal nucleus pulls ligands closer ‚Üí ligand field acts more strongly ‚Üí larger őĒ‚āí. [V(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ has őĒ‚āí significantly greater than [V(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≤‚Āļ because V¬≥‚Āļ is smaller and pulls water ligands nearer, intensifying the field.

Same charge: Co¬≥‚Āļ < Rh¬≥‚Āļ < Ir¬≥‚Āļ. This is why 4d and 5d metals almost always form low-spin complexes regardless of ligand.
